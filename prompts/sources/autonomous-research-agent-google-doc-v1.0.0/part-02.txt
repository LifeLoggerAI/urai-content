A strong source hierarchy is crucial. For scholarly and technical questions, the agent should begin with original papers, official documentation, standards, datasets, registries, and regulatory or government sources. For medicine and health, Cochrane recommends databases plus trial registries and increasingly regulatory sources and clinical study reports because unpublished or discrepant results often surface there. For public-company or market topics, official SEC filings are a direct primary source, and for novelty or competitive-IP questions, USPTO patent-search tools are direct primary sources. News and secondary commentary can help with recency, but they should not carry core empirical claims when primary material exists. 


Search strategy should be both broad enough to avoid premature narrowing and disciplined enough to stay reproducible. Cochrane recommends high-sensitivity search design, combining many terms with OR within concepts, using both free-text and controlled vocabulary, and peer reviewing the search strategy before final execution. Cochrane also warns that web results are less reproducible than database searches because search engines and localization change results, so the agent should log queries, dates, locales, interfaces, and filters used. PRISMA 2020 provides the reporting discipline for turning that search process into a transparent record. 


Extraction and synthesis should be structured, not ad hoc. Cochrane’s data-collection guidance emphasizes that reviews should treat studies rather than reports as the unit of interest, identify and link multiple reports of the same study, capture enough detail for transparent synthesis, archive the data for future reuse, and plan tables and figures early. GRADE complements that by rating confidence separately for important outcomes and by encouraging summary-of-findings tables for decision-makers. Together, those suggest a master prompt that forces the agent to produce an evidence matrix, not just prose. 


Verification should be explicit. Anthropic’s guidance on reducing hallucinations is especially useful here: allow the model to say “I don’t know,” ground factual work in direct quotes when possible, require citations for claims, and retract claims that cannot be supported. OpenAI’s tooling guidance likewise notes that, when tools are enabled, the model can automatically invoke web search for information beyond training cutoff. For repeated use, OpenAI also recommends versioning prompts, testing prompt changes, and rolling them out with evaluations instead of treating them as one-off text blobs. 


Handling unavailable or paywalled sources must remain lawful and explicit. Cochrane notes that copyright and licensing vary across jurisdictions and organizations, which means the agent should not attempt circumvention. Instead, it should use legal metadata and discovery tools such as Crossref, broad scholarly locators such as Google Scholar, registries, regulatory reviews, abstracts, author manuscripts, preprints, conference materials, cited reference chains, and other open alternatives available in the current run. If the full text cannot be accessed, the agent should say so, distinguish between abstract-level versus full-text evidence, and lower confidence accordingly. 


Ethics, law, and privacy should not be bolted on at the end. The Belmont Report’s core principles are respect for persons, beneficence, and justice; NIST’s AI RMF describes trustworthy AI in terms including validity, safety, security, accountability, transparency, explainability, privacy enhancement, and fairness; GDPR requires purpose limitation and data minimization and emphasizes privacy by design; and HIPAA applies to covered entities and business associates handling protected health information. For health-related efficacy or safety claims, the FTC expects competent and reliable scientific evidence and notes that randomized controlled human testing is generally the most reliable substantiation for such claims. A good master prompt should therefore instruct the agent to avoid overclaiming, minimize personal-data handling, and distinguish between exploratory evidence and substantiated conclusions.
